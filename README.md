Interpreting user needs begins with listening to what the client wants to accomplish rather than jumping to what should be built. Clients rarely come with a technical specification; they come with a business goal or a problem to solve. User stories make that translation possible by framing requirements from the user's perspective: "As a user, I want this feature, so that the benefit can be." This forces the team to evaluate features based on the value they deliver rather than how interesting they are to build. What makes user stories powerful is that they are small and testable. Acceptance criteria define exactly what "done" looks like before development begins, so the team never has to guess whether a feature meets the user's needs. Prioritization also becomes straightforward because each story is tied to a specific user benefit, and the Product Owner can rank them by business value rather than technical complexity.
My approach to development has shifted significantly through this course. I came in viewing software development as a linear process: gather requirements, design, build, test, deliver. What I have learned is that the model assumes a certainty at the start of a project that rarely exists. Requirements change, and clients often discover what they want by seeing what they do not want. Going forward, I plan to incorporate three Agile practices into my work: iterative delivery to get working features in front of stakeholders early and replace assumptions with real feedback; defining acceptance criteria before development begins to eliminate misaligned interpretations; and retrospectives to build continuous improvement into every project rather than treating lessons learned as an afterthought.
Being a good team member means more than writing clean code or meeting deadlines. It means communicating proactively, surfacing blockers early before they become crises, rather than protecting your status. It means taking ownership of the sprint goal rather than just your individual tasks, so that when unexpected changes occur, the whole team absorbs them together. And it means respecting the process even when it feels slow. The teams that skip planning and retrospectives to move faster are usually the ones that end up doing the most rework. Trusting the framework is part of working well with others.
